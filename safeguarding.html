Safeguarding

What is safeguarding?

Safeguarding is the act of protecting people's health, human rights, and wellbeing, and ensuring they are free from abuse, neglect, and harm. It's a collective responsibility to safeguard children, young people, and adults.

What can we all do?

We can all keep an eye out for each other and be aware of those in our community who may need support. Watch the video below for more information on protecting our community.

Warning Signs

Signs that someone is in need of help are not always clear and depend on the type of abuse, of which there are several. Read below for more specific help.

The deliberate use of force that causes harm, injury, or pain to another person.

Possible Signs:

  • Unexplained injuries, such as bruises, burns, or fractures
  • Frequent absences from school or social activities
  • Fear of physical contact or flinching

Ongoing emotional maltreatment intended to harm someone’s emotional well-being or sense of self-worth.

Possible Signs:

  • Low self-esteem or confidence
  • Withdrawn, anxious, or depressed behavior
  • Sudden changes in behavior (aggression or passivity)

Involvement in sexual activities without consent, including grooming, exploitation, and assault.

Possible Signs:

  • Age-inappropriate knowledge of sexual matters
  • Difficulty sitting or walking
  • Fear of certain people or places, avoiding situations

The failure to meet a person's basic physical and emotional needs, including food, shelter, medical care, and attention.

Possible Signs:

  • Poor hygiene or clothing that is inappropriate for the weather
  • Constant hunger, fatigue, or weight loss
  • Unattended medical needs or lack of proper supervision

Illegal or improper use of someone’s money, property, or resources.

Possible Signs:

  • Unexplained withdrawal from accounts or missing belongings
  • Sudden changes in financial circumstances
  • Lack of money for basic needs despite adequate income

Harassment, slurs, or similar treatment because of race, gender, age, disability, sexual orientation, or religion.

Possible Signs:

  • Isolation or avoidance of certain social groups
  • Exclusion from services or opportunities
  • Verbal abuse related to personal characteristics

Poor care or treatment within an institution or care setting due to systemic neglect or bad practices.

Possible Signs:

  • Lack of autonomy or choices for the individual
  • Inadequate staffing or unsafe environments
  • Strict or unnecessary rules and restrictions/li>

A pattern of behavior in a relationship that is used to gain or maintain power and control over an intimate partner. This can include physical, emotional, sexual, financial, or psychological abuse.

Possible Signs:

  • Unexplained injuries or frequent visits to the doctor or hospital
  • Withdrawal from social activities or isolation from friends and family
  • Sudden changes in personality, such as anxiety, depression, or low self-esteem
  • Partner seems overly controlling or excessively checks in on them

The exploitation of people through coercion, control, or threats, often involving forced labor, human trafficking, or domestic servitude.

Possible Signs:

  • Restricted freedom of movement, or being accompanied everywhere
  • Lack of identification documents, or someone else holding their personal documents
  • Signs of physical abuse, malnutrition, or untreated injuries

A person’s failure to attend to their own basic physical, emotional, or hygiene needs, which threatens their own health or safety. Often seen in vulnerable adults.

Possible Signs:

  • Poor personal hygiene, dirty or unkempt appearance
  • Malnutrition, dehydration, or significant weight loss
  • Living in unsanitary or unsafe conditions (hoarding, cluttered homes)
